Best Nonfiction Books for Young Adults Learning U.S. History

Teach the forgotten and overlooked stories of America's past with these top nonfiction books for young adults.

Who these books are for

  • Teenagers
  • Young adults
  • Students of U.S. history
  • Anyone looking for diverse perspectives on American history

Start here

If you only read one book about U.S. history for young adults, choose 'A Young People's History of the United States' by Howard Zinn.

Learning U.S. history as a teenager or young adult can be a transformative experience, shaping one's understanding of the modern world and America's role within it. But traditional textbooks often omit the stories and voices of marginalized communities, leaving important chapters of the past untold. These essential nonfiction books for young adults fill that gap, offering fresh perspectives and compelling narratives that bring U.S. history to life in a way that's engaging, accessible, and relevant to today's youth.

The collection

  1. An Indigenous Peoples' History of the United States for Young People

    Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz

    This book is a crucial, well-researched counter-narrative to the dominant Eurocentric history of the United States, offering a long-overdue Indigenous perspective on the country's past and ongoing issues.

    For: Middle grade and young adult readers, as well as educators seeking a more inclusive and accurate history curriculum.

    Strengths: Accessible adaptation of a seminal academic work, engaging young readers with a rich narrative while providing ample context, images, and discussion materials.

    Skip if: While the adaptation makes the complex history more approachable, the text can still be dense and heavy for some younger readers.

    Comparable: Other books in the collection that explore alternative histories and social justice, such as 'March' or 'The Underground Railroad', share this book's goal of challenging dominant narratives and promoting critical thinking.

    “Gripping, tightly written, and packed with facts traditional textbooks and historical accounts neglect to cover.”

    Buy on Bookshop.org

  2. Count Of Monte Cristo

    Alexandre Dumas

    This epic tale of betrayal, revenge, and redemption, spanning decades and continents, showcases Dumas' masterful storytelling and world-building.

    For: History buffs, literary enthusiasts, and anyone seeking a rich, immersive, and complex narrative experience.

    Strengths: Dumas crafts a labyrinthine plot filled with memorable characters, vivid descriptions, and historical context, all while exploring themes of justice, morality, and the human condition.

    Skip if: The lengthy, detailed nature of the story may overwhelm some readers, particularly with its slow-burning first half.

    Comparable: Fans of sprawling historical fiction like Victor Hugo's Les Misérables will appreciate the scope and ambition of The Count of Monte Cristo.

    Buy on Bookshop.org

  3. A Young People's History of the United States

    Howard Zinn

    A Young People's History of the United States offers a radical, people-centered perspective on America's past, giving voice to marginalized groups and challenging dominant narratives.

    For: High school students, history enthusiasts, and anyone seeking a more inclusive understanding of US history.

    Strengths: Zinn's engaging storytelling, meticulous research, and empathetic approach bring the struggles and triumphs of diverse populations to life, creating a nuanced, empowering narrative.

    Skip if: The book's dense, academic style and focus on political and social movements may not appeal to readers seeking a lighthearted or character-driven historical account.

    Comparable: Readers who appreciate Howard Zinn's historical works may also enjoy other progressive, people-focused histories like Roxanne Dunbar-Ortiz's An Indigenous Peoples' History of the United States.

    “s History that we have all been waiting for.”

    Buy on Bookshop.org

  4. A Young People's History of the United States Revised and Updated

    Howard Zinn

    A Young People's History of the United States offers a refreshingly inclusive and nuanced perspective on America's past, humanizing marginalized groups and challenging traditional narratives.

    For: This book is ideal for young adults, educators, and anyone seeking a more balanced understanding of US history.

    Strengths: Zinn's engaging storytelling and accessible prose make complex historical events relatable for a general audience, while his focus on underrepresented groups provides a much-needed corrective to mainstream accounts.

    Skip if: Some readers may find Zinn's radical political views and critiques of American imperialism alienating or overly one-sided.

    Comparable: Other books in the collection that share this focus on social justice and humanizing marginalized voices include The New Jim Crow and How the Other Half Lives.

Frequently asked questions

What makes these books great for young adults learning U.S. history?
They offer diverse perspectives, engaging storytelling, and thought-provoking insights that help young readers understand America's complex past.
Will these books cover all periods of U.S. history?
While not exhaustive, these books provide a comprehensive overview of key events, eras, and themes throughout U.S. history, from pre-Columbian times to the present day.
