Healing Isnt Linear: Grief's Uneven Path

By Darryl Purdie · 7 min read

Your phone is already buzzing before your feet hit the floor. The inbox yawns open. The world demands attention before you have given yourself permission to exist.

This is not the way it was meant to be.

The Principle of Grounded Presence

Ancient spiritual traditions across every wisdom path agree on one non-negotiable reality: before you can engage the world, you must first be present to yourself. This is not self-indulgence. It is spiritual survival.

Stillness is not the absence of movement-it is the presence of something deeper.

The practice invites you to begin with a profound simplicity: moments of intentional, embodied awareness before the momentum of the day takes hold. Not your phone. Not your to-do list. Just you, breathing, arriving.

Why This Moment Matters

Neuroscience tells us that intentional awareness begins to shift how your nervous system responds to the world. But more importantly, this practice respects your humanity. It is long enough to matter, gentle enough to sustain.

Transformation does not require heroics. It requires showing up-again and again, with honesty rather than ambition.

The Three Movements

Each session follows three gentle movements: arrival into the body, a short breath or awareness practice, and a single sentence of gratitude or intention. This trinity creates a container that is spacious enough for honesty and structured enough to be repeatable.

Over time-not overnight, but over weeks-you will notice small shifts. The morning panic softens. The reactivity to the first difficult moment diminishes. You begin to respond from a place of rootedness rather than reflex.

The Ripple Effect

A grounded morning does not guarantee a perfect day. But it does create a reserve. When the afternoon challenge comes, as it always does, you have something to draw upon. Not frantic energy, but a quiet centre that does not depend on circumstances.

This is the doorway. Through daily practice, you begin to discover that the life you long for is not found in adding more. It is found in returning to what was always present.
